Research Statement

A redesigned academic homepage centered on research themes, methods, and collaboration.

My work examines how people carry experiences between home and work, how they recover from demands, and how everyday transitions shape both well-being and performance.

Core Question

How do everyday experiences accumulate to influence employee functioning from one day to the next?

Applied Relevance

Findings can inform healthier work design, recovery-supportive practices, and more sustainable employee experiences.

01

Research Agenda

Three connected streams organize my current work.

Stress, recovery, and next-day functioning

Understanding how daily demands affect recovery and subsequent well-being, behavior, and performance.

Commuting as a transitional experience

Examining the commute as a meaningful bridge between non-work and work domains.

Non-work roles and engagement

Exploring how volunteering and other roles outside work contribute to energy, meaning, and involvement at work.

02

Approach

My research emphasizes within-person processes and practical implications.

Daily methods

I use diary and experience sampling approaches to capture fluctuations in employee experiences across days.

Context-sensitive theory

I examine how work and non-work contexts jointly shape affect, recovery, and performance-related outcomes.

Practice-oriented questions

Projects are motivated by questions that matter for healthier routines, transitions, and employee support systems.
