Industrial-Organizational Psychology

Studying the moments around work that often get overlooked.

Tracing how stress, recovery, and life outside work shape the workday.

My research centers on the transitions surrounding work: the commute in, the recovery period after, and the non-work roles that continue to influence employee energy, affect, and engagement.

Current Lens

I use daily diary and experience sampling approaches to capture how small shifts in routines and context accumulate into meaningful differences in well-being.

Methods Daily diary, within-person designs
Topics Recovery, commuting, volunteering
Goal Practical insight for healthier work lives

About Me

I am a graduate researcher at Virginia Tech specializing in industrial-organizational psychology. My work examines how employees carry experiences between life domains and how those patterns influence recovery, safety, and performance across days.

I am especially interested in the ordinary parts of a workday that rarely feel central, but often explain why people arrive depleted, engaged, distracted, or ready to recover.

What I’m Exploring

A few recurring questions that currently organize my research agenda.

01

Recovery after daily strain

How people unwind, detach, and regain energy after demanding work and non-work experiences.

02

Commuting as a transition period

The commute can amplify stress, shape emotion, and influence how employees begin the day.

03

Meaningful roles beyond work

Volunteer experiences and other non-work roles can either restore people or create new demands.
